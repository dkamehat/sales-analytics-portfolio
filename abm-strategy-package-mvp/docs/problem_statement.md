# Problem Statement: Account Prioritization Is Often Trapped in Meetings

## 1. Summary

ABM strategy and account tiering are important decisions about where to allocate sales, marketing, and leadership attention.

However, in many organizations, account prioritization is not operated as a reusable decision workflow. It often depends on recurring meetings, implicit criteria, and individual judgment.

The result is not necessarily bad intent or poor capability. The problem is that the decision process itself is not clearly designed.

## 2. Common symptoms

### 2.1 Criteria shift from meeting to meeting

One discussion emphasizes revenue size. Another emphasizes growth potential. Another emphasizes relationship strength, strategic fit, or delivery risk.

Each criterion may be valid, but if the criteria are not written down, the team repeats the same debate in the next cycle.

### 2.2 Analysis remains meeting material

Sales analysts and BizOps operators may prepare spreadsheets, dashboards, or SQL outputs.

But if those outputs are not connected to a decision function, the work remains supporting material rather than reusable strategy logic.

### 2.3 Consensus becomes expensive

Without explicit decision rules, meetings can become a search for an acceptable landing point rather than a structured review of evidence, trade-offs, exceptions, and next actions.

### 2.4 Tiering does not always create action

An account may be labeled Tier A, B, C, or Hold, but the next action, owner, success signal, and review date may remain unclear.

In ABM, the tier itself is less important than the play it triggers.

### 2.5 Decisions are hard to review later

If the team cannot explain why an account was promoted, demoted, held, or deprioritized, it cannot improve the prioritization logic over time.

Strategy should be reviewed and corrected, not only decided once.

## 3. Root cause

The root cause is that account prioritization is often treated as a discussion rather than an operating workflow.

| Surface problem | Underlying cause |
|---|---|
| Meetings feel repetitive | Decision logic is not reusable |
| Analysis keeps expanding | Inputs are not mapped to decisions |
| Stakeholders disagree | Criteria and exception rules are implicit |
| Decisions do not lead to execution | Actions are not attached to decisions |
| Learning does not accumulate | Review and audit logs are missing |

## 4. What this MVP tries to solve

This MVP does not claim to produce the perfect ABM strategy.

It tries to convert account prioritization into a reusable package:

```text
Input: account profile, stakeholder map, engagement signals, fit, potential, risk
Logic: prioritization, exception rules, counterarguments, review conditions
Output: proposed tier, reason, recommended play, review date
Action: next step, owner role, success signal
Audit: decision record, change history, learning loop
```

## 5. Desired state

The goal is not to remove meetings.

The goal is to change what meetings are used for.

| Before | After |
|---|---|
| Discuss criteria from scratch | Review exceptions against known criteria |
| Depend on implicit judgment | Use explicit drivers and counterarguments |
| Analysis ends as a slide | Analysis triggers a decision and action |
| Decide and move on | Set a review date and success signal |
| Rely on memory | Improve through logs and templates |

## 6. Use cases

- Account tiering
- ABM campaign selection
- Strategic account review
- Executive engagement prioritization
- Dormant account reactivation
- Expansion account mapping
- Sales resource allocation

## 7. Out of scope

- Building a predictive model
- Integrating with CRM/SFA systems
- Using real customer data
- Replacing human approval
- Solving organization politics

The MVP focuses only on the operating design of account prioritization.
